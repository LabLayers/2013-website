An Element's Fingerprint

Purpose:
To see how spectroscope can be used to identify different elements.

A line spectrum of a particular element is considered to be the 'fingerprint' of that element because no two elements emit the same wavelength. They are as unique as a human fingerprint, but easily identifiable.

Materials & Procedures

Materials:
Spectroscope, Gas Bulbs

Procedure:
1) Take one of the spectroscopes and a set of colored pencils (ROYGBIV)
2) Go to one of the gas lamps. If it is not on, carefully turn it on.
*) Do not touch any part of the glass bulb or the area where it connects into the lamp box.
3) With the room darkened, look through the narrow end of the spectroscope while you like up the small slit at the opposite end with the center of the gas bulb. Make sure that you hold the spectroscope horizontally.
4) You should see specific, bright lines of color that will be different for each gas bulb since each bulb is a differerent element. Each colored line in the spectrocope is over a number. This number represents the wavelength of that lignt in anfstroms (Ã…).

Spectroscope Readings

The line spectra of the classroom lights are grouped together yet seperated by solid chunks of color in an orderly fashion. The other elements often do seem not have any specific order or predictable pattern.

Sunlight, on the other hand, fills the entire spectrum without any dark spots.

The Neon Gas bulb contains a mixture of frequencies. This element emits a very red light because there is a stonger reading of the red frequencies.

The light is being caused by a close circuit of electrons. These gasses react to an excess of electrons by generating visible light wavelengths.

Conclusion

I learned how to properly use a spectroscope to measure the wavelengths of different elements.

An error that may have affected this experiment include interference from outside light sources (such as sunlight.) This will cause extra wavelenghts to show up, which will add false data to the results.

Another specific errort that may have affected is the setting of labatory itself. The students had to rush and take turns in order to get the experiment done in a timely manner. This will lead to potential mistakes.

A mistake that may have affected this experiment include inaccurate human readings, as this will negatively effect the accuracy of the results.

We now know that the sun is made of hydrogen and helium because of the spectroscope. Scientists can observe the line spectrums of the sun, and match them up with elements in the similar way to how fingerprint readers work.

This experiment can be improved with the use of a digital spectrometer. We will be able to go into more detail with this experiment and see the spectrum from ultraviolet to infrared (and beyond.)
