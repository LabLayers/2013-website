Striking It Rich

Turning bronze into silver and gold.

Procedure

  • 1 Obtain three shiny pennies.
  • 2 A small beaker in your lab tray contains of granulated zinc and zinc chloride solution. CAUTION: Zinc chloride can damage skin! If any of it gets on your skin, immediately flush with cold tap water.
  • 3 Using tongs, carefully lower two clean copper pennies (2 for each student) into the solution in the beaker. DO NOT drop the coins into the solution! Avoid causing a splash. Make sure the coins are touching the granulated zinc.
  • 4 Cover the beaker with a watch glass.
  • 5 Light the Bunsen burner and place it under the wire stand. Remember Bunsen burner Safety!
  • 6 Gently heat the solution until it just begins to bubble, continue gentle bubbling for approximately five minutes. DO NOT ALLOW the solution to boil vigorously or become heated to dryness. You will notice a change in the appearance of the pennies during this time.
  • 7 The third penny is a CONTROL, an untreated penny needed for comparison.
  • 8 Fill a second beaker with cold tap water.
  • 9 With tongs, remove the two pennies (2 for each student) from the solution. Place them both in the beaker with cold tap water. Turn off the burner but do not discard the zinc chloride solution. (When cooled, return the beaker with the solution to your lab tray)
  • 10 Using tongs, remove the coins from the beaker of water. Gently tap dry with a paper towel. Set one silver coin aside for comparison with the UNTREATED penny.
  • 11 Grab the second dry silver penny by its sides with the tongs and briefly heat the in the burner flame, gently moving it back and forth, as shown by your teacher. Heat only until you observe a change. DO NOT OVERHEAT.
  • 12 Immediately immerse the heated coin and tongs in the beaker of cold tap water.
  • 13 Remove the penny from the beaker and gently dry with a paper towel.
  • 14 Show all three pennies to your teacher for lab credit.

Result

Colors

Compare the colors of the three coins—untreated, heated in zinc chloride solution only, and heated in the burner flame. Are the treated coins now look like other metals? If so explain.

Precious Metals

If someone says that a precious metal was made in this activity, which physical property(-ies) will scientifically prove, whether or not a precious metal was made?

Alloy Formed

What happened to the copper atoms originally present in the treated pennies? What is the name of the alloy you formed in this lab? Identify two or three practical uses for the alloy you have made in this activity.

Other Alloys

Give 3 examples of other alloys used in our daily lives, including which metals make the alloy and what are some of the uses of each alloy.
