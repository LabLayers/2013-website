Some characteristics of many ionic compounds include solubility in water and the ability to conduct electricity. Salt, baking soda, detergent, cornstarch, and copper chloride display these two properties and are types of iconic compounds.

Water solutions of covalent compounds do not conduct electricity. Based on this property, it is safe to assume that gelatin, sugar, magnesium oxide and example of covalent compounds.

Based on this evidence, ionic and covalent compounds are distinctly different in the way that they conduct electricity. Iconic compounds are great conductors, and covalent compounds are not. In addition, covalent compounds are not conductive because they share electrons, while ionic compounds allow the free flow of electrons.

All of the compounds that conducted electricity appear to show the same amount of conductivity because each ionic compound lit up the conductivity indicator to the same brightness.
